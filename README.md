

# What Are You For?
### Judgment, trust, and learning in the age of AI

**Live:** https://justinsteinmetz.github.io/WhatAreYouFor/

---

## What it is

A philosophical instrument in six cases. Each case presents a real documented event where AI has challenged or displaced human judgment, creativity, or authorship. Students encounter the case, read the argument for what was gained and what was lost, then take a position — not on AI in the abstract, but on what specifically is at stake in this specific situation.

The instrument is not about AI. It is about authorship, judgment, trust, responsibility, and purpose under conditions of AI abundance. The closing question is not: what can AI not do? It is: if a machine can do most things you can do, what remains worth becoming?

---

## The arc

The six cases are not interchangeable. They build.

| Case | Domain | The question it asks |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Creative Writing | Can you trust the relationship between a work and its claimed origin? |
| 2 | Medical Diagnosis | When AI is more accurate, what does the human act of diagnosis still do? |
| 3 | Legal Judgment | What is lost when the source of a judgment cannot be seen or challenged? |
| 4 | Visual Art | What is the relationship between labour, authorship, and the value of a made thing? |
| 5 | Human-AI Collaboration | What did you contribute that mattered? |
| 6 | Education | If collaboration is the future, what is school for? |

Cases 1–4 are largely about trust: can you trust the source? Case 5 is the hinge: what if replacement is the wrong model entirely? Case 6 inherits that question and takes it into the classroom.

## The six cases

### Case 1 — Creative Writing: The author who wasn't there
Clarkesworld magazine closed submissions in 2023 after being flooded with AI-generated stories — hundreds per day, technically competent, indistinguishable from human work. A Japanese literary novel reached the prize longlist before the AI method was disclosed. On rereading, judges could not identify the AI passages. But the novel was effectively withdrawn from serious consideration.

The central argument: when a forgery is revealed, the painting doesn't change. The brushstrokes are the same. It becomes worthless anyway — not because it was bad, but because the relationship between the object and its claimed origin has been broken. AI authorship works the same way. The disclosure changes the meaning of the reading experience retroactively. This suggests the authorship was always part of what was being experienced, even when the reader didn't know it.

The counterargument: if you loved the painting before you knew it was a forgery, the love was real.

### Case 2 — Medical Diagnosis: The doctor who sees more
Google's AI predicted cardiovascular risk factors from retinal photographs that human doctors could not see. In dermatology, AI diagnosis of skin cancer has repeatedly matched or exceeded board-certified dermatologists. In 2023, a radiology AI detected breast cancers that two independent radiologists had missed. In rural India and Thailand, it was the only diagnostic tool patients would ever have access to.

The central tension: accuracy versus the human act of witnessing illness. The purpose of medicine is to correctly identify and treat disease — the ethical case for AI is overwhelming. But a doctor who looks at a patient is also performing a human act: witnessing suffering, establishing trust, making a person feel seen.

### Case 3 — Legal Judgment: The algorithm that decided your future
COMPAS — used in US courts to predict reoffending likelihood — was found by ProPublica to be twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as future criminals and half as likely to falsely flag white defendants. A 2018 study found it performed no better than random members of the public given the same information in a paragraph.

The central tension: consistent bias may be more correctable than invisible bias. But a person sentenced partly on the basis of a score they cannot see, challenge, or understand has been denied something fundamental. The algorithm encodes historical injustice into future decisions. It doesn't just predict the future — it produces it.

### Case 4 — Visual Art: The image that won the prize
In 2022, Jason Allen submitted an AI-generated image to the Colorado State Fair fine arts competition and won first place. He had not hidden the method. The other competitors had spent weeks producing their work by hand. In 2023, illustrators documented a 30–40% reduction in freelance income as clients switched to AI generation.

The central tension: art is about the image, not the process — if viewers respond, the response is real. But AI compresses the labour of thousands of artists into a tool that requires none. What we value in art may be not just the image but the evidence of a specific vision trained across thousands of hours of practice.

### Case 5 — Human-AI Collaboration: The new thing that doesn't have a name yet
This case does not describe an AI replacing a human. It describes something genuinely new: work that neither party could produce alone, and whose authorship cannot be fully located.

When a student recognises that the AI's explanation misses the real problem, when a designer sees that ten generated options solve nothing the client actually needs — the human contribution is not the production. It is the judgment that precedes and shapes it. The machine absorbs the cost of acting on the insight. The insight requires a human.

**The distinction that matters:** one student asks AI for an essay and submits it. Another asks for ten possible arguments, rejects eight, combines two, discovers a contradiction, and rewrites the conclusion. The first produced work. The second did something harder to name — and harder to see from outside. That invisibility is the problem.

**The scar tissue formulation:** the lazy AI essay has no scar tissue. It is complete. Nothing is missing. No argument trails off into uncertainty. It reads like something that was never in trouble. The worked collaboration has scar tissue — abandoned ideas, contradictions, revisions, moments of uncertainty, changes of direction. Struggle leaves marks. Smoothness leaves none.

The collaboration case is persuasive. That is part of the problem. Students who feel validated by it have probably not asked the question hard enough.

### Case 6 — Education: The essay that wrote itself
ChatGPT can pass the bar exam, the US Medical Licensing Exam, and MBA finals at Wharton and Minnesota Law. In 2024, 53% of UK university students surveyed had used AI for assessed work. Of these, 72% believed it had helped them learn; 41% believed it had replaced the learning they would otherwise have done. Both groups were probably right.

The current education debate asks: did the student write it? That question is already obsolete. The future question is: what did the student contribute that mattered? If AI can produce the visible outputs of learning, what evidence of learning remains?

## How it works

Students move through all six cases sequentially. For each:

1. **The case** — narrative, documented quote or statistic, argument for and argument against
2. **What happened here?** — three positions: something gained, something lost, or both simultaneously
3. **What specifically was lost?** — four concrete options, each naming a different value at stake
4. **One sentence** — the student's actual position in their own words

At the end: a closing question — *if a machine can do most things you can do, what remains worth becoming?* — a free-text response, and a summary record of all six positions side by side.

Nothing is stored. No login required. Approximately 20–30 minutes.

---

## Closing discussion questions

Built into the instrument's final screen:

- Look at where people disagreed. Which case produced the most different answers? Why that one?
- Is "AI can do it better" the same as "AI should do it"? What's the distinction?
- If AI can perform the visible outputs of learning, what evidence of learning remains?
- Case 5 asks: what did you contribute that mattered? Not what AI couldn't generate — AI can generate almost anything. Ask the room to answer that question about something they've actually made with AI. Where does their judgment change the work? Where does it not?
- The closing question — what did people write? The instrument ends on: *what remains worth becoming?* That question has no answer the AI can generate for them.

## Classroom notes

**Time:** 20–30 minutes for the instrument. Discussion is open-ended.

**Grade:** Grades 11–12. KMK Themenfeld 8 — Science and Technology. Also relevant to Themenfeld 1 — The Individual and Society.

**Facilitation:** No teacher framing required before students begin. The instrument is self-contained. The most productive classroom moment is the record screen — six positions from each student laid out simultaneously. Disagreements on Case 3 (Legal Judgment) and Case 5 (Collaboration) tend to be the sharpest.

**On Case 5:** The scar tissue formulation is the most memorable language in the instrument. It tends to dominate discussion. Watch for it pulling gravity away from the other cases. The correction is the discussion question: what did you contribute that mattered? Make students answer it about their own specific experience, not about the concept in the abstract.

**On the closing question:** *What remains worth becoming?* is not a question about AI's limits. It is a question about human purpose. Students who answer it by listing things AI can't do have not understood it. The question is not about capability. It is about significance.

**The productive tension to preserve:** Case 1 asks whether authorship matters. Case 5 asks what happens when authorship becomes genuinely hard to locate. Do not resolve that tension. It is the problem students are inheriting.
